Heinrich Brunn (1822–1894) The Institute of Classical Archaeology in Munich is home to a vast collection of photography. Starting in 2016 digitization of this collection has been underway, with the results made open to the public via the Arachne database. The photo negatives, photoprints , photo cards, glass plates and large-format slides can themselves be viewed in the photo archive (Fotothek) of the Institute of Classical Archaeology.

The photo archive today houses more than 100,000 pictures and is officially part of the Museum of plaster casts (Museum für Abgüsse München, MfA). The establishment of the photography collection dates back to the initiative of the archaeologist Heinrich Brunn. When he accepted the the position of Department Chair in 1865, he had two conditions: the founding of an archaeological seminar as well as a collection of plaster casts with an accompanying body of photography. The first picture dates back to 1871. It shows relief and statue fragments and parts of the architectural ornamentation from the Nereid Monument.

The Nereid monument in the British Museum, London In the beginning Heinrich Brunn set up the archive in his private apartment. For the first couple of years students could access the photos here, until both the collection of photography and the plaster casts were moved to the ground floor of the Hofarkaden on Galeriestraße 4 in Munich.

During the initial phase the archive counted merely 120 pictures. Only towards the end of the 19th century did the inventory increase substantially. In the year 1894 alone almost 200 photos were added. In addition to this Heinrich Brunn bequeathed 4,555 pictures to the institute once he passed. Another 6,500 were added under his successor Adolf Furtwängler. By 1929 the archive had expanded from 17,300 to 28,300 photos. Friedrich Hauser's collection was especially important. In 1917 he gifted all of his private books and photos to the institute.

An air strike in WWII destroyed many plaster casts and a small fraction of the photo archive. The rate at which new pictures were being acquired slowed down substantially during the war and finally came to a halt in 1943. Only in 1948 did the collection again start to grow. A year later the Institute for Classical Archaeology moved together with all the photos into the old administrative building of the National Socialist Party on Königsplatz, which today is known as the Haus der Kulturinstitute.

By the early years after the war the institute was in possession of close to 46,000 pictures. This includes numerous collected editions of photography such as "Photographische Einzelaufnahmen antiker Sculpturen" by P. Arndt und W. Amelung, which had been purchased between 1888 and 1947. In 1986 the archive was reorganized by Michael Pfanner. The old inventory system was replaced by one following the example of the German Archaeological Institute in Rome.

in the Capitoline Museums, Rome During the 1980’s and 1990’s photos from other institutes and museums were systematically purchased. In addition a number of gifted collections were added, among them ca. 200 pictures by the photographer Walter Hege, which he left to the institute in 1995. In addition a collection from the Bavarian Academy of Sciences for the study of ancient urbanism has been on permanent loan since the 1990’s. One of the largest additions is Paul Zanker's collection of photos of ancient portraits. It was donated in 2013 and is currently being digitized by student assistants.

Using the archive online

Arachne allows one to browse for individual pictures belonging to the Munich collections as well as the ancient objects, buildings etc. that are depicted on said photos. The negative numbers are comprised of a number ranging from 1 to 76166 and, for photos acquired after 1986, of the year, e.g. 86/123. The abbreviation FM (Fotothek München) is included to indicate the location of the negative. The negative numbers for the three main groupings of photography are as follows:

Total inventory:
FM-year/number, e. g. FM-12345 (before 1986) or FM-86/1234 (after 1986)
Donated collections:
FM-abbreviation for specific collection; e.g. FM-PZ00001 (PZ for Paul Zanker)
Reproductions:
FM-R-negative, e. g. FM-R-FA12-34 (Negative from the Forschungsarchiv für antike Plastik in Cologne)

Copies of photos that are property of the photo archive Munich can be ordered for publications. Reproductions cannot be ordered; in this case you must directly contact the respective owner.
